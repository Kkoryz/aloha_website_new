01 · State of Resort Wear 2026

Resort wear is no longer just what you pack for vacation. It is travel-led lifestyle commerce.

Force 01
Travel Has Become the Lifestyle
The $11.6T travel economy has permanently expanded who buys resort wear, when they buy it, and how long they wear it after the trip ends. The customer is no longer buying for a single trip — they are building a travel wardrobe.
Force 02
Social Commerce Has Collapsed the Funnel
A Reel or a tagged post now drives more trial than a season of wholesale orders. Shopify data shows AI-attributed orders grew 11× in 2025. Brands without a content system cannot compete for attention from social-first shoppers.
Force 03
Inventory Risk Has Never Been More Consequential
In a market defined by cautious buyers and tariff uncertainty, overbuying an untested print is the fastest way to end a new resort label. Low MOQ test-and-reorder is the correct operating model.
The Core Judgment
The brands that win in 2026 will be those that build around the end customer's full journey — from destination inspiration to post-trip repeat purchase — rather than around seasonal production calendars.
What This Means for Aloha & Co.
The strongest position is not factory. It is resort wear brand-building partner — helping boutique owners and emerging labels turn a print idea into a retail-ready capsule, then reorder the winners.

09 · Practical Playbook

Six Steps to a Retail-Ready Resort Wear Launch

#StepWhat to do
01Define the Customer Before the ProductAnswer: Who is the buyer? What is the price point? What is the use case? Is the brand selling destination, print, fabric, or lifestyle? Write the positioning sentence before briefing the print.
02Build the First Capsule Around One Print StoryCommit to 1 print story, 2–3 colorways, 3–5 hero styles, and 1 fit block per product type. Suggested mix: aloha shirt + pull-on short + dress or cover-up + matching set + swim item.
03Match the Capsule to the Sales ChannelA resort boutique needs easy merchandising. An Instagram label needs a hero drop. A surf shop needs function and UPF callouts. A gift shop needs destination story and giftable packaging.
04Prepare Production Like a BrandTech pack with flat sketch and measurements, repeat-ready print file, fabric decision by use case, full sample approval checklist, care label copy, and retail product descriptions.
05Use Low MOQ to Reduce Inventory RiskMOQ 50 per style is enough to equip 3–5 boutiques and test DTC response. Set a reorder trigger before you ship: if 70% sells through in 8 weeks, reorder immediately.
06Build Product Pages Before Bulk ProductionUse approved samples to build product pages with fit notes, lifestyle imagery, size charts, and fabric composition. Test ad creative before committing to larger runs. Demand signals are real data.

11 · Risk Map

Where Resort Wear Brands Fail — and How to Avoid It

High
SKU Overload
Launching 15+ styles in the first capsule destroys focus in production, photography, retail display, and the buyer pitch.
Cap the first capsule at 3–5 styles. Add SKUs only after first sell-through data is in.
↗ % of styles with >50% sell-through in 90 days
High
Generic Print Identity
Launching with stock prints or trend-copied graphics creates no brand recognition, no reorder loyalty, and no retail story.
Invest in one original print story before sampling. The print IS the brand in resort wear.
↗ Unaided brand recall in target channel
High
Fit Issues at Scale
Skipping or rushing sample approval causes construction problems, size inconsistencies, and return spikes after the bulk order arrives.
Run full sample approval: fit, print placement, wash test, stitch quality, label check. No shortcuts.
↗ Return rate by reason; target <5% fit-related
Medium
Inventory Pressure
Overbuying an untested print or style locks cash, creates markdown pressure, and can end a label before it has real sell-through data.
Use MOQ 50 for the first run. Treat it as paid market research. Reorder only proven winners.
↗ Days of inventory remaining; target <90 days
Medium
Retailer Confusion
A complex line sheet, too many colorways, or unclear merchandising guidance causes boutique buyers to pass — even if they like the product.
Deliver a one-page tearsheet per style, a visual display guide, and a "buy the story" bundle recommendation.
↗ Wholesale close rate; target >40% of pitched buyers
Medium
No Reorder Infrastructure
When a style sells out and a reorder should be placed, many small brands have no lead-time plan, no fabric hold, and no retailer communication ready.
Set reorder triggers (e.g. <20% inventory remaining) at launch. Pre-negotiate fabric minimums.
↗ Time from sell-out signal to reorder; target <2 weeks

12 · Metrics That Matter
Sell-Through Rate
Units Sold ÷ Units Bought × 100
≥ 65% at 12 weeks
Primary health signal. Tells you which styles deserve a reorder.
Best-Selling Print
Revenue + Units by Print Code
1 clear leader per drop
Identifies the print that earns reorder investment.
Gross Margin
(Revenue − COGS) ÷ Revenue × 100
≥ 55% DTC / ≥ 35% wholesale
Minimum viable margins by channel determine whether the model scales.
Return Rate & Reasons
Returns ÷ Orders × 100 + reason log
< 5% fit-related returns
Return reason data is the fastest feedback on fit accuracy and sizing.
Retailer Reorder Rate
Reordering buyers ÷ All buyers × 100
≥ 40% at season end
The strongest proof that the line merchandises well on the retail floor.
Days of Inventory Remaining
On-Hand Units ÷ Daily Sales Rate
< 90 days per style
Longer cycles lock cash and increase markdown risk. Track per style.
